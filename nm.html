About the course
This course is scheduled to be taught Spring ’15 at SHC. Students will have had one course on Object Oriented Programming and Calc 2. I wanted a programming language that was easy to install across platforms, free, and has full featured plotting to display graphics. For our primary programming language, I've decided to go with the Python 3.4. There is a fantastic Python installer from Continuum Analytics which includes all the popular science packages.
Projects
Each of the below will be the basis for a project. A source file will be provided with TODOs outlining tasks for the student to complete. Edit the file and compress your source with any output. Bonuses will be available for each project for going above and beyond. Examples include: additional feature implentation (faster algorithm, animation, user interactivity), a Julia or Clojure implementation of the assignment.

Final Project: Trefethen's list of 13 classic papers in applied mathematics.

Each student will select a paper from the list below (requests for approval of a paper not on the list must be made by mid-term). The student will report (1) First, information he/she had obtained about the historical context of the paper, including biographical information about the author(s) and a plot of citations as a function of time. (2) A mathematical discussion of some of the central ideas of the paper. (3) The results of Python experiments conducted to illustrate some of the properties of the algorithm under discussion.

Research articles can be intimidating. Even experienced researchers fight with them. Here is some advice on How to Read.

Wesley Pietri will be reviewing the paper: J.W. Cooley and J.W. Tukey, "An algorithm for the machine calculation of complex Fourier series," Math. Comp., 19 (1965), pp. 297–301.


Edgar Nah will be reviewing the paper: R. Courant, K. Friedrichs, and H. Lewy, "On the partial difference equations of mathematical physics," IBM J. Res. Develop., 11 (1967), pp. 215–234.


Kathleen Kuehl will be reviewing the paper: A.S. Householder, "Unitary triangularization of a nonsymmetric matrix," J. Assoc. Comput. Mach., 5 (1958), pp. 339–342.


Mary Catherine McKinley will be reviewing the paper: C.F. Curtiss and J.O. Hirschfelder, "Integration of stiff equations," Proc. Nat. Acad. Sci. U.S.A., 38 (1952), pp. 235–243.


C. de Boor, "On calculating with B-splines," J. Approximation Theory, 6 (1972), pp. 50–62.


R. Courant, "Variational methods for the solution of problems of equilibrium and vibrations," Bull. Amer. Math. Soc., 49 (1943), pp. 1–23.


Jade Bourgeois will be reviewing the paper: G. Golub and W. Kahan, "Calculating the singular values and pseudo-inverse of a matrix," J. Soc. Indust. Appl. Math. Ser. B Numer. Anal., 2 (1965), pp. 205–224.


A. Brandt, "Multi-level adaptive solutions to boundary-value problems," Math. Comp., 31 (1977), no. 138, pp. 333–390.


M.R. Hestenes and E. Stiefel, "Methods of conjugate gradients for solving linear systems," J. Research Nat. Bur. Standards, 49 (1952), pp. 409–436.


Kaitlin Carpenter will be reviewing the paper: R. Fletcher and M.J.D. Powell, "A rapidly convergent descent method for minimization," Comput. J., 6 (1963/1964), pp. 163–168.


G. Wanner, E. Hairer, and S.P. Nørsett, "Order stars and stability theorems," BIT, 18 (1978), no. 4, pp. 475–489.


N. Karmarkar, "A new polynomial-time algorithm for linear programming," Combinatorica, 4 (1984), no. 4, pp. 373–395.


L. Greengard and V. Rokhlin, "A fast algorithm for particle simulations," J. Comput. Phys., 73 (1987), no. 2, pp. 325–348.
